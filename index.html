How to take awesome portrait photographs!

Time: One hour
Awesome rating: 110% awesome

Do you struggle with navigating photography sessions? Here is a step-by-step guide on how to take awesome portrait photographs! These are perfect for going alongside public profile pieces, or other journalistic works.

  • Things You'll Need

  • Camera
  • Subject
  • Cool Setting!

  1. Set up your camera.

    Adjust the settings how you see fit to best accentuate your subject. It is sometimes necessary to utilize a slow ISO speed to create a warmer color contrast.

    Portrait photograph of subject Emelia that was captured by adjusting the camera settings.

  2. Adjust your lighting settings.

    Avoid harsh sunlight, overcast days can work in your advantage. Use your flash to fill in any shadows that harsh sunlight might create, but don’t rely on your flash all the time. Natural lighting is the way to go.

    Subjects Addy and Kathleen photographed in natural lighting.

  3. Make your subject feel comfortable.

    Get to know them, make them feel comfortable in front of the camera, and try to capture their true emotions. Give them something to fiddle with if their hands are out of the shot, because this can help ease any anxieties/tension they may be experiencing.

    Subject Maddie laughing during a portrait session.

  4. Choose a frame for your shot.

    There are three main frame choices for portrait photography: Head-and-Shoulders, The Body Shot, and the "Environmental" Portrait. Use the "Rule of Thirds" whenever aligning your frame.

  5. Have subject maintain eye contact.

    Unless you are specifically going for no eye contact for aesthetic purposes, eye contact is the key to a good portrait photograph. Always focus on the eyes while photographing.

    Subject Fia's eye during a portrait session.

  6. Adjust your focus settings between photographs.

    Ensure that you are manually, or automatically, adjusting the focus between each photograph so that they come out clear and concise. The subject should be the main point of focus in each picture.

    Camera focused on Subject Madi.

  7. Post-process your photos.

    Using an editing software of your choice, edit your photographs afterwards to make color adjustments, sharpen features, etc.

    Edited version of a portrait photograph of subject Madi. The highlights, shadows, saturation, and contrast were all edited to create a more dramatic, focused feel.

  8. Remember: Photography is an art form, not a science.

    While there are "rules" in place, remember that there are times when it is appropriate to— and where one should— break the rules. Get creative, and let your artistic expression show.

    Portrait photograph of subject Maddie that doesn't necessarily follow all of the rules, but is still an effective shot!
